%------------------------- Problem 1 -----------------------

\begin{problem}
	\includegraphics[scale=0.8]{1.png}
	\hfill
\end{problem}

\begin{solution}
Cosider the following limit, we have 
\begin{align*}
	\lim_{z \to 0}\abs{z f(z)}	
	&= \lim_{z \to 0}\abs{z}\abs{f(z)} \\
    &\leq \lim_{z \to 0}\abs{z}\frac{1}{\sqrt{\abs{z}}} \\
	&= \lim_{z \to 0}\sqrt{\abs z}\\
	&= 0,
\end{align*}
and so $\lim_{z \to 0} \abs{zf(z)} = 0$. 
I particular we then have that $\lim_{z \to 0} zf(z) = 0$ since $0$ is
the only complex number with zero modulus.

Now, we can construct a holomorphic extension of $f$ on $\Delta$.
(Note that this is the proof of an alternative characterization of 
the Riemann Removable singularity theorem that we saw in class).

Let $h: \Delta \to \C$ be defined by 
\[
	h(z) = \begin{cases}
		z^2f(z) & z \neq 0  \\
		0 & z = 0.
	\end{cases}
\]
Notice that $h$ is holomorphic on $\Delta^\star$ as the product of two
holomorphic functions on $\Delta^\star$. 
We show that $h$ is also holomorphic at $z = 0$. We have 
\[
	h'(0) = \lim_{z\to 0} \frac{z^2f(z) - 0}{z} 
	= \lim_{z \to 0} zf(z)  = 0,
\]
where the last equality follows by the argument above. And so $h$ is
holomorphic at $0$ and in particular $h'(0) = 0$.

Since $h$ is holomorphic at $z= 0$ it has a power series expansion
about $z = 0$
\[
	h(z) = c_0 + c_1 z + c_2 z^2+ c_3 z^3 + \cdots 
\]
However, by definition $c_0 = h(0) = 0$ and $c_1 = h'(0) = 0$ and so
we actually have 
\[
	h(z) = c_2 z^2 + c_3z^3 + \cdots 
\]
Recall that for $z \neq 0$ we have
\[
	f(z) = \frac{h(z)}{z^2} 
	= c_2 + c_3 z + c_4 z^2 + \cdots
\]
In other words, the power series
\[
	c_2 + c_3 z + c_4 z^2 + \cdots
\]
agrees with $f(z)$ for all $z \neq a$ in $\Delta^\star$. However, this power series ia
also manifestly holomorphic on all of $\Delta$. And so we have found a
holomorphic function which extends $f$ to $\Delta \to \C$. 


\end{solution}

%------------------------- Problem 2 -----------------------

\begin{problem}
	\includegraphics[scale=0.8]{2.png}
	\hfill
\end{problem}

\begin{solution}
Let $g(z) = f(1/z)$. Let us understand what kind of singularity occurs
at $g(0)$. If $g$ had a removable singularity at $0$ then we would
have that $g$ has a power series expansion about $z=0$ as $g(z) = c_0
+ c_1z + c_2z + \cdots$ and then in particular $g(0) = c_0$ some
finite value. However, this then implies that $f(z)$ is bounded for
large $z$. And then, as an entire function, this would imply that $f$
is constant. In other words $f(z) = b$ for some $b \in \C$, a special
case of the desired form. 

Now suppose $f$ is non-constant. We cannot have a removable
singularity for $g$ at $z=0$. Consider what happens if $g$ has an
essential singularity at $z = 0$. In this case we can apply the
Casorati-Weierstrass theorem; we have that the image under $g$ of any
neighbourhood of the origin $D_\ep(0)$ is dense in $\C$. By definition 
$g(D_\ep(0)) = f(\C \setminus D_{1/\ep}(0))$ is dense in $\C$. 
Now, recall that $f$ is holomorphic and so is, in particular, an open map.
That is the image of some other disc $f(D_\delta(0))$ is open in $\C$ 
and so must intersect $f(\C \setminus D_{1/\ep}(0))$. However, for
sufficiently small $\delta, \ep$ we have that $D_\delta(0)$ and $\C
\setminus D_{1/\ep}(0)$ are disjoint, yet their images under $f$
intersect. This means there are two distinct $z_1, z_2 \in \C$ with
$f(z_1) = f(z_2)$, contradicting the injectivity of $f$.
And so $g$ cannot have an essential singularity at $z = 0$. 

If $f$ is nonconstant, we are left with a pole at $z=0$ for $g$. That
is the Laurent series for $g$ at $z = 0$ is given by \[
g(z) = \frac{a_{-k}}{z^k} + \frac{a_{-k+1}}{z^{k-1}} + \cdots + a_0 +
a_1 z + a_2 z^2+ \cdots
\]
for some $a_i \in \C$ and with not all of the $a_i = 0$ for $i < 0$. 
Now since $g(z) = f(1/z)$ we have a Laurent series for $f(z)$ at $z=0$
by plugging in $1/z$ into the series above. That is, near $z =0$ we
have
\[
	f(z) = a_{-k}z^k + a_{-k+1}z^{k-1} + \cdots a_{-1}z + a_0 
	+ \frac{a_1}{z} + \frac{a_2}{z^2k} + \cdots 
\]
However, we have that $f$ is entire, and so is in particular
not singular at $z= 0$. Thus we have $a_i = 0$ for all $i \geq 1$. That
is, 
\[
	f(z) = a_0 + a_{-1}z + \cdots a_{-k}z^k.
\]
Moreover, since $f$ is entire this series holds everywhere on
$\C$ (has infinite radius of convergence). \wg{I think I see this
intuitively, but note to self to try and think about this using
Hadamard}

We have shown that $f$ is a polynomial, but recall that $\C$ is
algebraically closed. And so if $f$ is degree $k$ then it has $k$
roots in $\C$. That is, there are $k$ points in $\C$ where $f(z) = 0$.
However, it must be that $k=1$, otherwise we would have shown that $f$
is not injective. That is, we in fact have that $f$ is a degree $1$
polynomial
\[
	f(z) = a_0 + a_{-1} z,
\]
for some $a_0, a_1 \in \C$. 


\end{solution}

\begin{problem}
	
	\includegraphics[scale=0.8]{3.png}
	\hfill
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
	Recall that the residue of a meromorphic function $f: U \to \C$ at
	a point $p \in U$ is given by the $a_{-1}$ coefficient in it's
	Laurent expansion about $p$.
	For this question we determine the Laurent expansion of
	$\sin^3(z)$ about $z = 0$ by using power series that we already
	know.

	Recall the Taylor expansion of $\sin z$ about $z= 0$ is given by \[
		\sin z = z - \frac{z^3}{3!}	+ \frac{z^5}{5!} - \cdots
	\]
	And so in particular we have the following Laurent series
	expansion of $\sin^3(1/z)$ about $z = 0$
	\[
	\sin^3\left(\frac{1}{z}\right)	= \left[
		\frac{1}{z} - \frac{1}{3!}\frac{1}{z^3} + \frac{1}{5!} \frac{1}{z^5} - \cdots
	\right]^3
	\]
	Now, notice that the largest power of $z$ in this expansion is
	going to be $1/z^3$ with all other terms having a lower power of
	$z$. That is the coefficients of the Laurent series expansion is
	$a_n = 0$ for all $n \geq -2$. In particular $a_{-1} = 0$ and so 
	the residue of $\sin^3(1/z)$ about $z = 0$ is $0$. 

	Note that althought the residue of our function is $0$ at $z=0$,
	our function does have a singularity at $0$. In fact, the Laurent
	series expansion about $z=0$ has infinitely many terms with negative powers of
	$z$ and so the singularity at $z= 0$ is essential.


\end{solution}

\begin{problem}
	\includegraphics[scale=0.8]{4.png}
	\hfill
\end{problem}

\begin{solution}
Recall that $res_p(gf'/f)$ is the $a_{-1}$ coefficient in the Laurent
series expansion of $gf'/f$ about $p$. We determine this expansion.
Near $p$ we can write
\[
	f(z) = (z-p)^k h(z)
\]
where $h$ is some
holomorphic function on a neighbourhood about $p$ which does not
vanish on some neighbourhood about $p$ (this latter claim follows from
isolation of zeroes of holomorphic functions).
Applying usual differentiation rules gives $f'(z) = k(z-p)^{k-1} h(z)
+ (z-p)^k h'(z)$. And so,
\[
	\frac{f'(z)}{f(z)} = 
	\frac{k}{z-p} + \frac{h'(z)}{h(z)}
\]
where the latter term is holomorphic about $p$, since there exists a
neighbourhood about $p$ where $h(z)$ is non-vanishing. As an aside,
note then that we have just shown that $res_p(f'/f) = k = Mult_p(f)$. 

Now consider 
\[
	\frac{gf'}{f} = g(z) \left[
	\frac{k}{z-p} + \frac{h'(z)}{h(z)},
	\right].
\]
Since $g$ is holomorphic on $U$ it has a power series expansion about
$p$, say $g(z) = b_0 + b_1(z-p) + b_2(z-p)^2 + \cdots$. 
It follows then that
\[
	res_p(gf'/f) = ``a_{-1}\left(g(z)\left[
		\frac{k}{z-p} + \frac{h'(z)}{h(z)}
	\right]\right)'' = b_0 k = g(p)Mult_p(f).
\]

If in particular $g(z) = z$ then we have $res_p(zf'/f) = p Mult_p(f)$. 
And so the Residue formula gives \[
\int_{\partial D} \frac{z f'(z)}{f(z)} dz = 2\pi i \sum_{p \in D} p
Mult_p(f). 
\]
A weighted sum of points in an (appropriate) disc, weighted by the
order of vanishing of $f$ at those points. Note that only finitely
many terms of the sum on the right hand side of this expression are
non-zero.

\end{solution}
