%------------------------- Problem 1 -----------------------

\begin{problem}
	\includegraphics[scale=0.8]{1.png}
	\hfill
\end{problem}

\begin{solution}
We show that automorphisms of $\mathbb H$ are isometries by direct
calculation. Let $f \in PSL_2(\R)$, $z = x + iy \in \mathbb H$, and $w
= f(z)$. If we allow $\abs{dz}^2$ to denote $\abs{dz}^2 = dx^2 + dy^2$ then our
metric is $\frac{\abs{dz}^2}{y^2}$. To show that $f$ is an isometry we need
to show that 
\[
	\frac{\abs{dw}^2}{Im(f)^2} = \frac{\abs{dz}^2}{y^2}. 
\]
We have that $dw = f'(z) dz$ and so let us compute the derivative
$f'(z)$. Recall that $f(z) = \frac{az + b}{cz + d}$ for $a,b,c,d \in
\R$ and $ad-bc = 1$. Then using the quotient rule gives 
\begin{align*}
	f'(z) &= \frac{a}{cz+d} - \frac{c(dz+b)}{(cz+d)^2} \\
	&= \frac{acz + ad - acz - bc}{(cz+d)^2} \\
	&= \frac{1}{(cz+d)^2}.
\end{align*}
And so 
\[
	\frac{\abs{dw}^2}{\abs{dz}^2} = \frac{1}{\abs{cz+d}^2}.
\]
On the other hand let us compute $Im(f)^2$. 
We have 
\begin{align*}
	w &= \frac{az+b}{cz+d} \\
	&= \frac{(az+b)\overline{(cz+d)}}{\abs{cz+d}^2} \\
	&= \frac{(az+b)(c\bar z+d)}{\abs{cz+d}^2} && a,b,c,d \in \R \\
	&= \frac{ac\abs{z}^2 + adz + bc \bar z + bd}{\abs{cz+d}^2}.
\end{align*}
Now, notice that the denominator is a real number and that 
the only part of the numerator which may contain imaginary terms is
$adz + bc\bar z$. Consider 
\begin{align*}
	adz + bc\bar z &= ad(x+iy) + bc(x - iy) \\
	Im(adz + bc\bar z) &= ady - bcy \\
	&= y. 
\end{align*}
That is, 
\[
	\frac{Im(w)^2}{Im(z)^2} = \frac{1}{\abs{cz+d}^2} \frac{y^2}{y^2} = \frac{1}{\abs{cz+d}^2}. 
\]
We have thus shown that automorphisms of $\mathbb H$ are isometries of
the hyperbolic metric. 

Now let $f : \mathbb H \to \mathbb H$ be an isometry of the upper half
plane with the hyperbolic metric. 
We have that, $ds^2 = \frac{dx^2+dy^2}{y^2}$ which is a conformal
transform of the euclidean metric. Which means that its isometries
must be conformal. We have seen that the only conformal functions of
$\C$ are the holomorphic functions and the antiholomorphic functions.
An antiholomorphic function cannot be a function $\mathbb H \to
\mathbb H$. And so $f$ is a holomorphic function of $\mathbb H$. 

Now that we know $f$ is holomorphic, the calculation above shows that
$f$ must satisfy 
\[
	\frac{\abs{f'(z)}^2}{Im(f(z))} = \frac{1}{Im(z)}.
\]
If $f(x,y) = u(x,y) + iv(x,y)$ this expression gives us a condition on $v$. 

\end{solution}

\end{problem}

\begin{solution}
Let $p,q$ be two points of the upper half plane with the same real
part. If $\gamma$ is a curve in the upper half plane, the length of
$\gamma$ under the hyperbolic metric is given by contracting the
tangent vectors against the metric along the curve. That is if
$\gamma: [0,1] \to \mathbb H$ with $\gamma(0) = p$ and $\gamma(1) =
q$ then the hyperbolic length of this curve is 
\[
	L = \int_a^b \sqrt{g_{\gamma(t)}(\gamma'(t), \gamma'(t))} dt,
\]
where $g_{(x,y)}(u,v)$ means the contraction of tangent vectors $u,v$
in the tangent space at $p = (x,y) \in \mathbb H$. (I am using some
conceptual understanding from physics here, is this also the way one
would say this in metric geometry?)

If $\gamma$ is not the vertical line connecting $p,q$ then it moves a
nonzero amount parallel to the real line. For a fixed imaginary value,
the hyperbolic length of a horizontal line is given by a scaling of
its euclidean length. That is, any distance travelled in the
``real direction'' by $\gamma$ adds a positive value to the length of our curve.
In particular this means that the geodesic connecting $p,q$ is given
by the curve which does not travel in the real direction at all ---
this is namely the vertical line connecting $p,q$.

Now suppose that $p,q$ two points no longer lying on a vertical line.
We saw in the previous question that any automorphism of $\mathbb H$
is a hyperbolic isometry. In particular, it must bring geodesics to
geodesics.
Then, it is sufficient to show that there
exists an automorphism of $\mathbb H$ which brings $p,q$ to a vertical
line. For a given $p,q$ there's a unique semicircle meeting the real
axis at a right angle specified by three points in the complex plane
$p,q,x$ where $x \in \R$. By triple transitivity of the Mobius transformations, there's
then a unique Mobius transformation $M$ mapping $(x,p,q) \mapsto (0,
\tilde p, \tilde q)$ where $\tilde p, \tilde q \in \mathbb H$ and are
colinear.

A priori our transform $M \in PGL_2(\C)$, however, if we can show that $M \in
PSL_2(\R)$ then are done. Since $M \in GL_2(\C)$ it has determinant $D
\neq 0$. We can scale $M$ by any $\lambda \in \C$ to acquire an
equivalent transformation. But $\abs{\lambda M} = \lambda^2 D$,
and so scaling $M$ by $1/\sqrt{D}$ means that $M$ can be considered to
have determinant $1$ without loss of generality.


Recall that the automorphisms of $\mathbb H$ are a subgroup of
$PSL_2(\R) \leq PGL_2(\C)$ and so, we need to argue that the
transformation found above has real entires.



\end{solution}

\begin{problem}
	\includegraphics[scale=0.8]{3.png}
	\hfill
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
We found in problem $1$ that the automorphisms of the upper half plane
are exactly the automorphisms of the hyperbolic metric on the upper
half plane. We also know a biholomorphism between $\mathbb H$ and
$\Delta$ and so we can use this to compute a candidate metric on
$\Delta$ which is preserved under any automorphism of $\Delta$.

We saw in class a biholomorphism $\Delta \to \mathbb H$ via \[
	h(z) = i \cdot \frac{1+z}{1-z}.
\]
This map has holomorphic inverse $f: \mathbb H \to \Delta$ via 
\[
	f(z) = \frac{z-i}{z+i}.
\]
Now let us denote the hyperbolic metric on $\mathbb H$ by $ds^2 =
(dx^2 + dy^2)/y^2 = \abs{dz}^2 / y^2$. Then using the relation
$dw = f'(z) dz$ where $w = f(z)$ we can compute how the metric transforms under $f$. 
Using the quotient rule we get that 
\[
	f'(z) = \frac{2i}{(z+i)^2}. 
\]
And so \[
	\abssq{dz} = \frac{\abs{z+i}^4}{4} \abssq{dw}. 
\]

Now if $z = x + iy \in \mathbb H$, let us compute $y = Im(z)$ in
terms of $w$. 
We have that
\begin{align*}
	x + iy &= i \cdot \frac{1 + w}{1 - w} \\
	&= i \cdot \frac{1 - \bar w + w - \abssq{w}}{\abssq{1 - w}} \\
	y &= \frac{1 - \abssq{w} + Re(w - \bar w)}{\abssq{1 - w}} \\
	y &=  \frac{1 - \abssq{w}}{\abssq{1 - w}}.
\end{align*}
Note that if $a \in \C$ then $a - \bar a$ is a purely imaginary
number. 

Now we can compute a candidate metric 
\begin{align*}
	ds^2 &= \frac{1}{y^2} \abssq{dz} \\
	&= \left(\frac{\abssq{1-w}}{1 - \abs{w}^2}\right)^2 \frac{\abs{z+i}^4}{4} \abssq{dw}. 
\end{align*}
Now notice that ssince $w = \frac{z-i}{z+i}$ we have that 
$1 - w = \frac{2i}{z + i}.$
And so $\abssq{z+i} = 4/\abssq{1-w}$. 
Now, we are left with 
\[
	ds^2 = \frac{1}{\left(1 - \abssq{w}\right)^2} \abssq{dw},
\]
where $w \in \Delta$. This is our candidate metric on $\Delta$.

Recall that the automorphisms of $\Delta$ are of the form 
\[
	g(z) = e^{i \theta} \phi_a(z),
\]
for some angle $\theta$ and where $\phi_a$ is a Blaschke map.
If we show that rotations and all $\phi_a$ are isometries of our
proposed metric then compositions of such maps (i.e. all automorphisms
of $\Delta$) will also be isometries. 
Notice that the conformal factor of our candidate metric depends only
on $\abssq{w}$, the magnitude of our point in $\Delta$. Thus, it is
certainly preserved under rotations. 
The last thing to compute is whether the Blaschke maps all satisfy 
\[
	\frac{\abssq{\phi'_a(z)}}{\left(1 - \abssq{\phi_a(z)}\right)^2} = \frac{1}{\left(1- \abssq{z}\right)^2},
\]
for all $z \in \Delta$ and all $a \in \Delta$. 

\end{solution}

\begin{problem}
	\includegraphics[scale=0.8]{4.png}
	\hfill
\end{problem}
\begin{solution}
Let $f$ be as given in the question. First suppose that $f(0) = 0$.
We show that if this function has a nonzero fixed point then it is the
identity.
Recall the Schwarz lemma for such a function states 
\[
	\abs{f(z)} \leq \abs{z} \text{for all } z \in \Delta 
	\qquad
	\text{ and } 
	\qquad \abs{f'(0)} \leq 1,
\]
and if equality is achieved in either for some nonzero $z_0 \in \Delta$, then
$f$ is a rotation. 
Suppose $f$ has a fixed point, i.e. $f(z_0) = z_0$ for some $z_0 \in
\Delta$. But then we have 
$\abs{f(z_0)} = \abs{z_0}$ and so $f$ is a rotation, $f(z) =
e^{i\theta} z$. However, since $f(z_0) = z_0$ and $z_0 \neq 0$ it must
be that $e^{i\theta} = 1$, i.e. $f$ is the identity. 
That is, a nonidentity holomorphic $f$ with $f(0) = 0$ only fixes the origin.

Now let $f$ be general (i.e. drop the assumption $f(0) = 0$). 
I claim that if $f$ has more than one fixed point then it must be the
identity on $\Delta$. 
Suppose that $f$ has two distinct fixed points $f(a) = a$ and $f(b) =
b$. Now let $g: \Delta \to \Delta$ be defined by 
\[
	g = \phi_a \circ f \circ \phi_a^{-1},
\]
where $\phi_a: \Delta \to \Delta$ is the Blaschke map (the
automorphism of the disc) which exchanges $0$ and $a$. 
Since $f(a) = a$ this function satisfies $g(0) = 0$. 
Notice that $\phi_a(b)$ is also a nonzero fixed point of $g$. And
so by the argument above, $g$ is the identity.
Now conjugating $g$ by $\phi_a$ implies that $f(z) = z$ is the
identity (recall that $\phi_a\inv = \phi_a$). 
Thus, we have shown that if $f$ has two distinct fixed points then it
must be the identity on $\Delta$. In other words, a non-identity
holomorphic function on $\Delta$ has at most a single fixed point. 

We show that there exists a holomorphic function on $\Delta$ whose set
of fixed points is the empty set.
Let $x \in \R$ be nonzero and consider the Blaschke map 
\[
	\phi_x = \frac{z - x}{1 - xz}. 
\]
The fixed point equation $\phi_x(z) = z$ is equivalent to
\[
	0 = x(1 - z^2).
\]
However the solutions to this equation in $z$ are only for $z \in
\partial \Delta$. That is, this equation has no solutions in $\Delta$
and so $\phi_x$ has no fixed points in $\Delta$. 
Recall that $\phi_x$ is a holomorphic function on $\Delta$ since it is
an automorphism of $\Delta$. 


We have shown that if $f$ is non identity then it can only have a
single fixed point. The constant map $f: \Delta \to \Delta$ via $f(z) = a$ for
some $a \in \Delta$ has a fixed point at $a$.
If $f: \Delta \to \Delta$ is the identity restricted to the
disc has every point as a fixed point.

Overall, the possibilities for the set of fixed points are $\Delta,
\{a\}$ where $a \in \Delta$, and $\O$. 

\end{solution}

\end{problem}

\begin{solution}
First, we need to verify that the proposed $f$ has outputs to the
upper half plane. And then, if we can construct an inverse function,
then we have shown that the given function is a biholomorphism $\tilde
\Delta \to \mathbb H$ where $\tilde \Delta$ denotes the upper half disc. 

First we show that the proposed function outputs to $\mathbb H$.
Let $z \in \Delta$, we need to show that $Im(f(z)) > 0$.
Recall that $Im(f(z)) = (f(z) - \overline(f(z))) / 2i$. 
A quick computation gives that 
\[
	Im(f(z)) = 
	\frac{1}{2i}\left(f(z) - \overline{f(z)}\right) = -\frac{1}{2} Im(z) \left(1 - \frac{1}{\abssq{z}}\right). 
\]
However, recall that $z \in \Delta$ and so, $1 - 1/\abssq{z} < 0$.
Thus, $Im(f(z)) > 0$ and the proposed function outputs to $\mathbb H$
for all $z \in \Delta$, and in particular, for all $z \in \tilde
\Delta$. 

Now, we show that the proposed function has an inverse $\mathbb H \to
\Delta$. 
Let $w \in \mathbb H$, we want to show that there is a unique $z \in
\tilde \Delta$ such that $w = -1/2(z + 1/z)$. Rearranging this equation
gives 
\[
	0 = z^2 + 2wz + 1. 
\]
As a quadratic equation, this has two roots $z_0, z_1 \in \C$. 
Recalling then that we can factor our quadratic as $0 = (z -z_0)(z-z_1)$, 
the coefficients from the above give the following relations on
the roots
\begin{align}
	z_0 z_1 &= 1 \\
	z_0 + z_1 &= -2w. 
\end{align}
This first equation gives us that $z_0$ and $z_1$ are inverses of each
other. I claim that neither $z_0, z_1$ lie on $S^1$. Suppose $z_0 \in
S^1$ then equation $1$ would imply that $z_1 \in S^1$ and that $z_0,
z_1$ are conjugate to each other. But then their sum is real. And then
equation $2$ cannot be satisfied since $w \in \mathbb H$ and so $-2w$
has strictly negative imaginary part. Hence, neither $z_0, z_1$ lie on
$S^1$. 

Now, equation $1$ implies that one of the roots lies in $\Delta$ and
the other root lies in $\mathbb C \setminus \Delta$, since $z \mapsto
1/z$ exchanges the unit circle and its complement. Let's say that $z_0
\in \Delta$. 
Now I claim that equation $2$ can only be satisifed if in fact $z_0
\in \tilde \Delta$. 
If we write $z_0 = r e^{i \theta}$, $r < 1$ then $z_1 = z_0^{-1} =
1/r e^{-i \theta}$ (i.e. $z_1$ lies on the same ray as
$\overline{z_0}$ but with larger magnitude). And so if $z_0$ were to
lie in the lower half disc we would have that $z_0 + z_1$ has positive
imaginary part. But as noted earlier, $w \in \mathbb H$ and so $-2w$
has negative imaginary part. Thus, we must have $z_0 \in \tilde
\Delta$.


We have shown that given $w \in \mathbb H$ the equation $0 = z^2 + 2wz
+ 1$ has a unique solution in $\tilde \Delta$. In other words, 
we have shown that there exists a function $\mathbb H \to \tilde
\Delta$ which inverts the given $f$. Hence $f$ is a holomorphic
bijection, thus $f$ is a biholomorphism $\tilde \Delta \to \mathbb H$. 

\end{solution}
